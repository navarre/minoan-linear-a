## Interpretation

The loanword pattern supports the **language isolate hypothesis**:

1. **Place names**: Multiple Cretan place names pass from Linear A → Linear B → Greek. These are substrate words that survived the language transition. They prove Minoan was spoken at these locations.

2. **Commodity terms**: A few trade terms (cumin, possibly emmer) appear to be borrowed from Akkadian/Semitic — consistent with Bronze Age eastern Mediterranean trade networks. These are **cultural borrowings**, not evidence of genetic relationship.

3. **Religious terms**: The Luwian connection (ishassara) may represent **religious contact** with Anatolia, not linguistic kinship. Similar to how Latin borrowed Greek religious terminology.

4. **No systematic sound correspondences**: The loanwords are isolated borrowings, not evidence of regular phonological relationships that would indicate a language family.

## Key finding

The loanword directionality is significant:
- **Minoan → Greek**: Place names (the substrate layer)
- **Akkadian → Minoan**: Trade terms (eastern contacts)
- **Anatolian ↔ Minoan**: Religious terminology (bidirectional contact)

This trilateral contact pattern is exactly what an isolate language in a Bronze Age trade network would show.

## Confidence
**Provisional success.**
Place name identifications are secure. Trade term comparisons are plausible but rely on phonetic similarity that could be coincidental. Religious term comparisons are the weakest link.
