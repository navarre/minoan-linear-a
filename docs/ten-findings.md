# Ten Things We've Found About Linear A
### A computational research project by Matt Navarre — Heraklion, Crete
matt@navarre.ai | https://navarre.github.io/minoan-linear-a/

---

**1. The oldest prayer in Europe is partially readable.**
The libation formula — carved on stone offering tables at peak sanctuaries across Crete — can now be partially translated. *ja-sa-sa-ra-me* is almost certainly Luwian *ishassara* + possessive *-me*: **"My Lady."** *i-da-ma-te* combines Mount Ida with *mater*: **"Mother of Ida."** One sign — *301 — blocks the opening phrase.

**2. The sequence A-SA-SA-RA was written at Arkhanes before Linear A existed.**
Seal stones from the Phourni cemetery (~2000 BCE) bear the sign group A-SA-SA-RA — the same root found in the libation formula a thousand years later. Writing on Crete may have begun for religious purposes, not bookkeeping. The seal stones are here in this museum.

**3. The Anetaki ivory scepter (here in this museum) is the longest Linear A text ever found.**
Discovered in 2024 at the Knossos cult center, published by Kanta, Nakassis, Palaima & Perna in 2025. ~119 signs across an ivory circle and handle — far longer than any tablet. The full sign-by-sign transcription has not yet been published. When it is, it could transform our understanding of Minoan.

**4. Sign *301 appears on 48 documents — more often than the sign for grain.**
It functions as both a logogram (stamped alone on 32 nodules at Haghia Triada) and a syllabogram (in the libation formula and personal names). It has no equivalent in Linear B, meaning it represents a sound that Greek doesn't have. Solving *301 could unlock the oldest prayer in Europe.

**5. 70% of Linear A signs were inherited by Linear B, but only 1.4% of the words match.**
Of ~1,463 known Linear A words, only 21 have parallels in Linear B — and those are almost all personal names and place names. The scripts are related. The languages are not. This is the strongest evidence that Minoan is not Greek.

**6. We can now say what the Minoan language is NOT.**
Computational morphological analysis shows Linear A records an agglutinative language — suffixes stack 3-4 deep without changing the root. This rules out Greek (fusional) and Semitic (root-and-pattern). It resembles Hurrian typologically, but the core vocabulary has no Hurrian cognates. The language remains unidentified.

**7. "Labyrinth" is a Minoan word that has survived 3,500 years.**
Linear A *du-pu₂-re* ("lord/master") → Linear B *da-pu₂-ri-to* → Greek *labyrinthos*. The word originally meant "place of the lord" — the palace itself. Similarly, *ku-pa-ri* in Linear A likely became Greek *kyparissos* (cypress).

**8. The Minoan language probably survived a thousand years after its script died.**
Eteocretan inscriptions at Dreros and Praisos (7th–3rd century BCE) are written in the Greek alphabet but in a non-Greek, non-Indo-European language. Herodotus called these people "Eteocretans" — "true Cretans." No systematic comparison between Eteocretan and Linear A morphology has been published. This is an open research gap.

**9. A unified digital corpus of 1,880 documents is now freely available.**
Merging data from SigLA, lineara.xyz, and GORILA into a single searchable platform with a document gallery (773 inscriptions with drawings), sign explorer (315 sign types), interactive site map, and a 52-word glossary with confidence scores. All open source.

**10. This project is actively looking for collaborators and expert review.**
We welcome corrections, challenges, and contributions from scholars working on Aegean scripts. The full analysis — including hypothesis testing, a morphological grammar sketch, and the *301 deep dive — is available at the project website.
