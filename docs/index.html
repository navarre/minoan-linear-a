Major Discovery

The Longest Linear A Inscription Ever Found

In 2024, an ivory scepter bearing ~119 Linear A signs was recovered from the Anetaki plot at Knossos — a ceremonial cult center. Published by Kanta, Nakassis, Palaima & Perna in Ariadne (2025), this single artifact contains more text than most tablets combined.

~119 signs Longest known inscription
2 scribes Ring vs. handle show different hands
Dual script Linear A + Cretan Hieroglyphic
Ritual No numerals on ring — ceremonial, not accounting

The ring features 12 metope panels with quadrupeds, vessels with syllabographic ligatures (possibly acrophonic), textiles, and hides. The handle adds numerals and Cretan Hieroglyphic signs. This bridges administrative and religious writing for the first time.

Read the paper: Kanta et al. (2025), Ariadne Suppl. 5, 27–43

What is Linear A?

The Script

Linear A was used in Bronze Age Crete from approximately 1700–1450 BCE. It is the primary writing system of the Minoan civilization, predating the deciphered Linear B (Mycenaean Greek) by centuries.

Despite over a century of scholarship, Linear A remains undeciphered. The underlying language is unknown—it is not Greek, and its language family has never been conclusively identified.

Our Approach

This project applies computational methods to the corpus of ~1,400 known inscriptions. Rather than guessing translations, we build from the ground up:

  • Statistical frequency analysis
  • Positional pattern detection
  • Cross-site comparison
  • Hypothesis testing with confidence scores

Reproducibility over revelation. The math doesn’t lie.

The Corpus

Inscriptions survive on clay tablets, stone vessels, roundels, and other objects found across Crete and the Aegean. Two major categories:

  • Administrative: Accounting tablets recording commodities, quantities, and totals (mainly from Haghia Triada)
  • Religious: Libation formulas on stone vessels from peak sanctuaries and caves

Key Findings

Results from computational analysis: frequency analysis, information theory, cross-linguistic comparison, and morphological pattern detection.

Linear A is Natural Language

Word frequencies follow Zipf’s law with high confidence. This definitively rules out hypotheses that the script is a cipher, code, or purely symbolic system.

The Libation Formula

a-ta-i-*301-wa-ja   ja-sa-sa-ra-me   i-da-ma-te
“[I] dedicate [this to] My Lady, Mother of Ida”

Found on 29+ stone vessels from 16 sites across Crete. The most important religious text in Linear A, suggesting a mother goddess tradition that continued into Greek Rhea/Cybele worship.

Administrative System

Minoan palatial economy revealed through tablet analysis:

  • Commodity tracking: grain, oil, wine, figs, olives, barley, sheep, goats, aromatics
  • Arithmetic verification: ku-ro totals match item sums (77/127 documents validated)
  • Deficit tracking: ki-ro records shortfalls against expectations
  • Inter-palatial network: same names appear across multiple palace sites

Reduplication

Seven words show reduplication, suggesting intensification or morphological doubling:

  • ja-sa-sa-ra-me — intensification of root
  • qa-qa-ru — possible intensive
  • ja-di-ki-te-te — morphological doubling
  • a-sa-sa-ra-me — variant of ja-sa-sa-ra-me

Grammar Sketch

  • Word order: NAME . COMMODITY QUANTITY (administrative)
  • Cases: -te (dative/locative), -ja (adjectival/ethnic)
  • Enclitics: -me (possessive “my”)
  • Derivation: a- prefix, reduplication
  • Syllable structure: Predominantly CV and V
  • Vowel harmony: Absent (2.1%) — rules out Uralic/Turkic

Validate & Discover

Complete

Independently confirm known results, then find new patterns no one has published.

14 of 14 checkpoints
  • ku-ro = "total" confirmed (74 occurrences, 5.3× next candidate)
  • ki-ro = "deficit" confirmed — always follows ku-ro with smaller value
  • po-to-ku-ro = "grand total" confirmed from composite analysis
  • Libation formula: 3-tier structure (core → ja-sa-sa-ra-me → i-da-ma-te), 100% ritual-exclusive
  • HT administrative structure detected from data alone
  • Zipf’s law validated (exponent 1.103, R²=0.901) — confirms natural language
  • Morphological patterns: productive prefixes (a-, da-, ku-) and suffixes (-te, -ja, -me, -ro)
  • Paradigmatic sets: ta-na/ta-na-te/ta-na-su (same root, different case endings)
  • Word boundary preferences: vowels/stops initial (a 91%, qa 96%, ku 87%), liquids/nasals final (ro 95%, na 86%, ne 91%)
  • Site dialect variation: vocabulary overlap near zero (Jaccard 0.000–0.016), 88–100% site-exclusive words across 772 docs
  • Word classes: 24 administrative terms vs. 63 probable names separated by distributional co-occurrence with ideograms and numerals
  • Commodity distribution: FIC (figs) most common (49 docs), sign A301 on 33 docs, 313 fraction occurrences across corpus
  • Sign co-occurrence: 16% of common bigrams forbidden (no vowel gemination, nasal-liquid restriction, stop harmony avoidance) — 4,408 pairs
  • Compound words: ku-ro in 21 compounds, a- prefix dominates 8/10 largest families, productive suffixes -ta-na, -mi-na, -sa-ra identified

Reconciliation Changelog

The sign inventory was reconciled from two independent data files, fixing several errors in the Ventris grid mapping:

  • AB67: was ka (error) → corrected to ki (B067 = ki in Ventris grid)
  • AB47: was ki (error) → corrected to unknown (no Linear B parallel)
  • AB22: was mi → corrected to pi2 (distinct from AB73, the standard mi)
  • AB86: was unassigned → nwa (medium confidence, per Younger)
  • 20 signs added from second inventory: AB12/so, AB14/do, AB15/mo, AB32/qo, AB33/ra3, AB36/jo, AB42/wo, AB43/a3, AB52/no, AB62/pte, AB68/ro2, AB71/dwe, AB72/pe, and more

Inscription Sites

Linear A inscriptions have been found across Crete and the wider Aegean, from palace archives to mountain-top sanctuaries. View interactive map on SigLA

Haghia Triada

52 inscriptions

South-central Crete. The richest source of Linear A, yielding ~150 tablets (42 digitized). Primarily administrative/accounting documents recording commodities, personnel, and totals.

Zakros

14 inscriptions

Eastern Crete palace. Administrative tablets and ritual inscriptions.

Khania

12 inscriptions

Western Crete. Administrative records showing connections to other palace centers.

Phaistos

10 inscriptions

South-central Crete palace. Administrative tablets from the neopalatial period.

Ioukhtas

6 inscriptions

Peak sanctuary above Archanes. Stone libation tables with ritual formulas.

Syme Viannou

4 inscriptions

Mountain sanctuary in eastern Crete. Libation vessels spanning several centuries.

Other Sites

29 inscriptions

Knossos, Archanes, Malia, Petras, Psychro Cave, Palaikastro, Tylissos, and Aegean islands (Thera, Kea, Miletos).
