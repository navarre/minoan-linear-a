The oldest prayer in Europe — carved in stone, 3,500 years ago

a-ta-i-*301-wa-ja
ja-sa-sa-ra-me
i-da-ma-te
“[Sacred opening — one sign remains undeciphered]
My Lady,
Mother of Ida”

This is the libation formula — a prayer inscribed on stone offering tables at mountain-top sanctuaries across Bronze Age Crete. Priests poured wine, oil, and honey into carved depressions while reciting these words to the Minoan goddess. It appears on 86 documents from 16 sites, making it the most important text in Linear A.

We can read most of it. ja-sa-sa-ra-me is almost certainly Luwian ishassara + possessive -me — “My Lady.” i-da-ma-te combines Mount Ida with *mater — “Mother of Ida.” But one sign — *301 — has no equivalent in any known script. It appears on 48 documents, more often than the sign for grain. It represents a sound that Greek doesn’t have. Solve *301, and the prayer opens. Solve the prayer, and we’re closer to reading Minoan than anyone has been in 3,500 years.

The Undeciphered Script

Linear A was the writing system of the Minoan civilization on Bronze Age Crete, used from approximately 1800–1450 BCE. Despite over a century of scholarship, it remains undeciphered. The underlying language is unknown — our analysis shows it is not Greek, not Semitic, and most likely an agglutinative language isolate with religious borrowings from Luwian (Anatolian).

The total known corpus: 1,534 inscriptions with 7,574 signs (RILA 2025). This project has assembled 1,880 document records into a unified, searchable corpus.

What We Know

We can read the numbers (identical to Linear B). We know ku-ro means “total” and ki-ro means “deficit.” We’ve identified 52 words with varying confidence. We can trace the grammar: 6–8 case suffixes, agglutinative morphology, reduplication for plural, a suffix that distinguishes people (-ja) from things (-ra).

The libation formula gives us two Luwian loanwords, an agglutinative verb, and one devastating gap: sign *301.

3,500 Years of Silence

Around 1450 BCE, Linear A vanished. Every major site on Crete was destroyed. Only Knossos survived, and there the script was replaced by Linear B — the same signs, but writing Greek. The Minoan language may have survived for centuries as Eteocretan (“true Cretan”) in eastern Crete, written in the Greek alphabet but in an unknown tongue. The last traces fade by the 3rd century BCE.

The prayer on the stone tables is the closest we’ve come to hearing their voice.

What We’ve Discovered

It’s a Real Language

Mathematical analysis proves Linear A records a genuine spoken language, not a code, cipher, or symbolic system. Word frequencies follow the same statistical laws as every known human language. Whatever the Minoans were writing, they were writing language.

It’s Not Greek. It’s Not Semitic.

The Minoan language builds words by stacking suffixes — 3, 4, even 5 deep — without changing the root. This is called agglutinative morphology, and it rules out Greek (which fuses its endings) and Semitic languages (which weave vowels into consonant roots). The language has no known relatives. It may be the last echo of a language family that has otherwise vanished.

The Prayer

a-ta-i-*301-wa-ja   ja-sa-sa-ra-me   i-da-ma-te
“[Sacred opening] … My Lady … Mother of Ida”

Found on 86 stone offering tables from 16 sites across Crete. “My Lady” is a Luwian word (ishassara) borrowed into Minoan for religious use. “Mother of Ida” invokes the sacred mountain where Zeus was later said to be born. One sign — *301 — blocks the opening phrase.

52 Words, Partially Understood

We can read ku-ro (“total”) and ki-ro (“deficit”) because the arithmetic checks out — the numbers after ku-ro actually add up. We know the names of four Cretan cities. We know the word for “lord” (du-pu₂-re), which 3,500 years later became the Greek word labyrinth.

The Scripts Are Related. The Languages Are Not.

About 70% of Linear B signs were inherited from Linear A. But of the ~1,463 known Linear A words, only 21 have any parallel in Linear B — and those are almost all personal names and place names. Someone adapted the Minoan alphabet to write Greek, just as the Latin alphabet was later adapted for Turkish, Finnish, and Vietnamese.

“Labyrinth” Is a Minoan Word

Linear A du-pu₂-re (“lord, master”) became Linear B da-pu₂-ri-to, which became Greek labyrinthos. The word originally meant “place of the lord” — the great palace at Knossos. Similarly, ku-pa-ri in Linear A probably became Greek kyparissos (cypress tree). Every time you say “labyrinth,” you are speaking Minoan.

Writing Began with Prayer, Not Bookkeeping

The sequence A-SA-SA-RA — the root of the libation formula’s “My Lady” — appears on seal stones from the Phourni cemetery at Arkhanes, dated to roughly 2000 BCE. That’s 200 years before Linear A existed. The first writing in Europe may have been carved for the gods, not for counting grain.

The Longest Inscription Was Just Found

In 2024, archaeologists at Knossos discovered an ivory scepter with ~119 signs — by far the longest Linear A text ever found. Published in 2025, the full sign-by-sign transcription has not yet been released. When it is, it could transform our understanding of Minoan writing. The scepter is at the Heraklion Archaeological Museum.

Project Goals

1

Analysis Platform

In Progress

Statistical tools for the corpus. Frequency analysis, positional patterns, cross-site comparison, morphological detection. Open for other researchers.

  • 14 reproducible benchmarks with methodology and results
  • Zipf validation, entropy analysis, bigram/phonotactic analysis
  • Cross-site dialect variation analysis
  • Word class detection (admin terms vs. names vs. ritual)
  • Package tools for external use
  • Automated re-run against updated corpus data
2

Vocabulary & Grammar

Ongoing

Maintain a growing glossary with confidence scores and a grammar sketch. Evidence-based, falsifiable, honest about uncertainty.

  • 52-word glossary with confidence scores and evidence chains
  • 6–8 case suffixes identified with distributional evidence
  • Word boundary phonotactics mapped
  • Loanword directionality established
  • Expand glossary as corpus analysis deepens
  • Resolve sign *301
3

Scholarly Integration

In Progress

Cross-reference with GORILA, RILA 2025, published scholarship. Track discrepancies. Maintain provenance. Be useful to working scholars.

  • Data discrepancy report for expert review
  • Cross-reference report: SigLA vs lineara.xyz vs GORILA
  • Terminology page with standard definitions
  • Incorporate RILA Supplement 2025 (107 new documents, 427 signs)
  • Review by domain expert (Dr. Salgarella contacted)
  • Incorporate feedback from scholarly review
4

Open Data

Mostly Done

Everything open source, reproducible, documented. JSON files anyone can use.

  • All data in open JSON format (corpus.json, signs.json, glossary.json)
  • All analysis scripts in Python, open source on GitHub
  • 14 benchmarks with reproducible methodology
  • CC-compatible source data attribution
  • Write a proper README for external contributors
5

Cross-Script Comparison

Not Started

Systematic comparison of Linear A signs with related and contemporary writing systems. Visual similarity, structural parallels, and shared sign values.

  • Map Linear A signs to Linear B counterparts with confidence levels
  • Compare with Cretan Hieroglyphic (the predecessor script)
  • Investigate connections to Cypro-Minoan and Egyptian hieratic
  • AI-powered visual similarity search across writing systems
  • Identify shared logograms and potential cognates
6

Translation

Not Started

Move from pattern recognition to reading. Start with administrative texts where arithmetic provides a check, then attempt ritual texts.

  • Translate a complete accounting tablet with arithmetic verification
  • Translate a roundel inscription with word-by-word gloss
  • Identify 3+ divine names with probable meanings
  • Produce a connected reading of the libation formula
  • Attempt a non-formulaic ritual text
7

Decipherment

The Goal

The ultimate objective: determine the language behind Linear A. This may require new archaeological discoveries — a bilingual inscription, a larger corpus, or a Rosetta Stone moment. Every step above brings us closer.

  • Identify the language family (or confirm isolation)
  • Assign phonetic values to all common syllabograms with high confidence
  • Read administrative texts fluently
  • Read ritual texts with reasonable confidence
  • Produce translations that withstand peer review
  • A new archaeological find could change everything overnight

Inscription Sites

Linear A inscriptions have been found across Crete and the wider Aegean, from palace archives to mountain-top sanctuaries.

Haghia Triada

1,167 documents

South-central Crete. The richest source of Linear A, yielding 1,167 documents including tablets, nodules, and roundels. Primarily administrative/accounting documents recording commodities, personnel, and totals.

Zakros

53 documents

Eastern Crete palace. Administrative tablets and ritual inscriptions.

Khania

244 documents

Western Crete. Administrative records showing connections to other palace centers.

Phaistos

79 documents

South-central Crete palace. Administrative tablets from the neopalatial period.

Ioukhtas

16 documents

Peak sanctuary above Archanes. Stone libation tables with ritual formulas.

Syme

12 documents

Mountain sanctuary in eastern Crete. Libation vessels spanning several centuries.

Other Sites

309 documents across 49 sites

Knossos, Archanes, Malia, Petras, Psychro Cave, Palaikastro, Tylissos, and Aegean islands (Thera, Kea, Miletos).
