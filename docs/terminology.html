Terminology & Definitions

Standard terms used in Linear A epigraphy and this project. Following GORILA, SigLA, and CDLI conventions.

Physical Objects

Every Linear A inscription exists on a physical object. These are the types of objects that carry writing.

Tablet ~394 in corpus administrative
A flat clay slab, usually rectangular (5–10 cm), inscribed with a stylus while wet. The primary administrative record type. Lists commodities, quantities, names, and totals. Often has two writable sides (designated a and b). Think of it as a spreadsheet carved in clay.
Example: HT 1 — a grain accounting tablet from Haghia Triada listing recipients and totals.
Roundel ~151 in corpus administrative
A small flat clay disc, roughly circular, with seal impressions on both faces and a short inscription around the edge. Functioned like a receipt — the seal identifies the authorizing party, the text records what the transaction was for. Usually very short (1–3 signs).
Example: HT Wa 1001 — a grain roundel from Haghia Triada.
Nodule ~886 in corpus administrative
A small lump of clay pressed around a string or cord, then stamped with a seal. Like a wax seal on a letter — it authenticates who authorized the tied/sealed package. Often has just 1–2 signs (sometimes a single logogram). The most numerous object type by count, but the least text per object. Mostly from Haghia Triada.
Example: HT Wc 3006 — a nodule with a single sign impression.
Sealing ~12 in corpus administrative
Similar to a nodule, but pressed against a surface (a door, a box, a container) rather than around a string. Proves that something was sealed shut by an authorized person. The clay preserves the seal impression and sometimes a few signs.
Stone Vessel ~107 in corpus ritual
A carved stone bowl, cup, or offering vessel used for ritual purposes — specifically for pouring libations (liquid offerings) to the gods. These carry the libation formula (a-ta-i-*301-wa-ja ja-sa-sa-ra-me). Found at peak sanctuaries and caves across Crete. NOT administrative — purely religious.
Example: IO Za 2 — a libation vessel from the Iouktas peak sanctuary.
Libation Table subset of stone vessels ritual
A specific type of stone vessel — a flat stone table with a depression for pouring liquid offerings. Found at mountain-top sanctuaries (Iouktas, Psychro, Syme Viannou). The libation formula appears most consistently on these objects. Often carved in a more monumental style than other vessels.
Clay Vessel ~76 in corpus various
A pottery jar, cup, or container with signs scratched or painted on it. Could be a maker’s mark, an owner’s name, or a label of contents. Usually very short (1–3 signs). Includes cups, jugs, and other ceramic forms.
Pithos ~6 in corpus various
A large clay storage jar, sometimes human-sized. Signs on pithoi are typically short — a name, a commodity label, or a capacity mark. These were the Bronze Age equivalent of warehouse containers.
Metal Object ~23 in corpus ritual/prestige
Bronze or gold items with inscriptions. Includes axes, rings, pins, and other prestige objects. The famous Arkalochori axe from a cave sanctuary has Linear A inscribed on its blade. These are typically votive (religious offering) objects.
Lames ~18 in corpus administrative
Thin, narrow clay tablets shaped like a short ruler. A subtype of tablet used for brief records. Sometimes called “bars” in older literature.
Graffiti ~3 in corpus various
Signs scratched on walls, floors, or stone surfaces. Not formal documents. May be practice writing, casual marks, or ad hoc records.
Label ~3 in corpus administrative
A small clay tag, likely attached to a container or bundle. Similar function to modern shipping labels.

Writing System Components

The building blocks of Linear A as a writing system.

Sign
Any single mark in the writing system. Not the same as a letter. A sign can represent a syllable (syllabogram), a whole word/concept (logogram), a number, a fraction, or a transaction marker. Linear A has ~356 distinct sign types.
Sign Type
An entry in the sign inventory — a specific sign as defined in GORILA. Each sign type has a catalog number (e.g., AB01, A301). Multiple physical occurrences across different documents share the same sign type.
AB81 (ku) is the most common sign type: 175 occurrences across 132 documents.
Sign Occurrence / Attestation
A specific instance of a sign type on a specific document. “AB81 occurrence #3 on HT 1” is one attestation. The full corpus has 4,935 sign occurrences (in SigLA) or 7,574 (per RILA 2025 across all known inscriptions).
Syllabogram 164 in sign inventory
A sign that represents a syllable (a unit of sound). Most Linear A syllabograms represent consonant+vowel (CV) pairs like da, ro, pa, te. Phonetic values are hypothesized from visually similar Linear B signs (which are deciphered). There are 164 known syllabograms, of which 57 have high-confidence readings.
AB01 = da, AB81 = ku, AB02 = ro. So ku-ro is written with two syllabograms.
Logogram / Ideogram 117 in sign inventory
A sign that represents a whole word or concept, not a sound. Used for commodities (GRA = grain, VIN = wine, OLE = oil), animals (OVIS = sheep, BOS = cattle), people (VIR = man), and other categories. Some signs function as both syllabogram and logogram depending on context.
AB30 functions as logogram FIC (figs, 65 times) AND syllabogram ni (48 times).
Fraction 14 in sign inventory
A sign representing a fractional quantity. Unique to Linear A — NOT found in Linear B. Designated A701 through A713 (J, E, F, K, L, W, etc.). Their exact values are debated but probably represent halves, thirds, quarters, etc. The most common is A707/J (112 occurrences), possibly = 1/2.
Transaction Sign 61 in SigLA corpus
A specialized sign that marks the type of transaction on an administrative document. Appears at the beginning of entries. No Linear B parallel exists. AB04 (te) is the most common, often preceding commodity lists.
Numeral
A number sign. Linear A uses a decimal system: vertical strokes = units, horizontal bars/dots = tens, circles = hundreds, rayed circles = thousands. The numeral system is well understood and matches Linear B.
Erasure 100 in SigLA corpus
Traces of a previously cancelled sign still visible on the writing surface. The scribe made a mistake, smoothed the clay, and wrote over it. Sometimes the erased sign can still be identified; often it cannot.

Textual Units

Word / Sequence
A group of consecutive syllabograms forming a lexical unit, separated by word dividers (dots or vertical bars) on the original object. In transcription, syllables are separated by hyphens: ku-ro, ja-sa-sa-ra-me, a-ta-i-*301-wa-ja. The corpus contains 1,211 unique words.
Reading
The phonetic or semantic value assigned to a sign or word. Readings are hypothetical for syllabograms (derived from Linear B parallels) and reasonably secure for logograms (identified from context and Linear B). A “sure reading” means the SigLA editors are confident; an uncertain reading is marked with [?].
Transliteration
Converting a Linear A text into Roman letters using the hypothesized phonetic values. 𐙐-𐘁 → ku-ro. This is NOT a translation — it’s just a way to represent the sounds.
Transcription
Recording the original sign forms, either as Unicode characters (𐘀 block) or as GORILA catalog numbers (AB01, A301, etc.). More precise than transliteration because it doesn’t require phonetic interpretation.
Unicode
The international standard for encoding text characters digitally. Linear A has its own Unicode block (U+10600–U+1077F), assigned in 2014. This means Linear A signs can be typed and displayed as actual characters: 𐤀 rather than just catalog codes like “AB01.” Not all Linear A signs have Unicode codepoints — only the ~80 most common syllabograms and some logograms are encoded. lineara.xyz uses Unicode extensively; SigLA uses catalog codes.
Variant
A different written form of the same sign. Scribes at different sites or time periods drew the same sign differently. For example, AB131 (wine) has three known variants in SigLA: AB131a, AB131b, AB131c. Similarly, AB21 (sheep) has male and female variants: AB21m, AB21f. Variants are critical for palaeographic analysis (studying scribal hands) but represent the same linguistic unit. In our corpus, some documents appear as variants too: HT 113 ter is a third copy of the same text as HT 113.
Canonical ID
The standardized, unique identifier we assign to each document or sign across all sources. Different databases use different naming conventions: SigLA writes HT 1 (with space), lineara.xyz writes HT1 (no space), the old corpus sometimes uses different numbers entirely. The canonical ID follows GORILA conventions (with spaces) and serves as the universal lookup key. Our canonical ID map links 1,880 document IDs across all sources.
Findspot
The exact archaeological location where an inscription was discovered. More specific than “site” — a findspot might be “Portico 11 and Room 13” within the site of Haghia Triada. Findspot data helps determine which documents were used together in the same administrative context. Available for 591 documents in our corpus (from lineara.xyz).

Document Identification

Document ID / Siglum
The unique identifier for an inscription, following GORILA conventions. Format: SITE NUMBER[face].
HT 1 = Haghia Triada, document 1
HT 10a = Haghia Triada, document 10, side a
KH Wa 1001 = Khania, roundel (Wa), number 1001
ZA 10b = Zakros, document 10, side b
IO Za 2 = Iouktas, stone vessel (Za), number 2
Site Code
Two-to-four letter abbreviation for the findspot: HT = Haghia Triada, KH = Khania, PH = Phaistos, ZA = Zakros, KN = Knossos, MA = Mallia, ARKH = Arkhanes, IO = Iouktas, PE = Petras, PK = Palaikastro, THE = Thera, KE = Kea, SY = Syme, TY = Tylissos, MI = Miletos.
Object Type Code (in document ID)
A letter code embedded in the document ID indicating the object type: Wa = roundel, Wb = nodule (flat-based), Wc = nodule (other), Za = stone vessel, Zb = clay vessel, Zc = metal object, Zf = miscellaneous, Zg = graffito. Documents without a type code are usually tablets.
Face Designation
Multi-sided objects have face suffixes: a = recto/front, b = verso/back. Some documents have Greek-letter face designations (α, β, γ) for sub-faces of roundels and nodules. HT 10a and HT 10b are two sides of the same physical tablet.

Reference Works

GORILA
Recueil des inscriptions en linéaire A by Louis Godart & Jean-Pierre Olivier (1976–1985). 5 volumes. THE definitive catalog of all Linear A inscriptions. Every inscription has a GORILA number. Vol 1: tablets pre-1970. Vol 2: nodules/roundels pre-1970. Vol 3: 1975–76 finds. Vol 4: other documents. Vol 5: addenda, corrigenda, sign tables. Published by École française d’Athènes.
RILA Supplément 1 (2025)
Update to GORILA by Maurizio Del Freo & Julien Zurbach. Adds 107 new documents and 427 signs discovered/published between 1985–2023. Brings total corpus to 1,534 documents, 7,574 signs. Published by École française d’Athènes, 2025. Available on Academia.edu.
SigLA
The Signs of Linear A: a palaeographical database by Ester Salgarella & Simon Castellan (2020–). Focuses on palaeography (how signs look when written). Contains 772 documents with hand-drawn sign images, SVG bounding boxes, and searchable attestations. Focuses on palaeographically useful documents; does NOT include most nodules, stone vessels, or clay vessels. sigla.phis.me
lineara.xyz
Open-source corpus explorer by Robert Hogan, based on George Douros’s tabulation of GORILA. Contains 1,722 documents including nodules, stone vessels, clay vessels, and metal objects that SigLA excludes. Has Unicode transcriptions, GORILA image references, and Younger’s commentary. lineara.xyz | GitHub
Younger’s Linear A Texts
Website by John G. Younger (University of Kansas) with phonetic transcriptions of 1,077 inscriptions (75.5% of GORILA). The standard reference for transliterated readings. Uses a slightly different numbering system from GORILA in some cases. people.ku.edu/~jyounger/LinearA/
