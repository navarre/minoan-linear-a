# Verifier R10 — Steelmanning the Greek Hypothesis

## Task
Construct the strongest possible case **for** the Greek hypothesis for Linear A, using the project's own anti-Greek arguments plus Mosenkis-style materials, then assess how strong that case actually is.

## 1. The strongest arguments FOR Greek

A good steelman should begin by acknowledging the non-trivial evidence that makes a Greek reading attractive at all.

### G1. Linear A and Linear B are structurally related scripts
This is the strongest starting point for any Greek hypothesis.

- Linear B descends from Linear A
- many signs are graphically shared
- some sign values appear transferable
- the scripts were used on the same island and in overlapping administrative traditions

A Greek advocate can argue:
> if Linear B writes Greek and inherits much of the Linear A signary, it is at least possible that Linear A also encoded an early Greek or para-Greek variety under a different orthographic regime.

That does not prove Greek, but it does make Greek worth testing rather than dismissing out of hand.

### G2. There are real LA–LB lexical overlaps
The project now notes **21 LA-LB parallels**, not just a handful.

Even if many are names or place names, the Greek advocate can argue:
- shared toponyms are not trivial
- shared lexical islands may preserve older Greek or pre-Mycenaean Greek contact layers
- if more than one or two administrative or cult terms also align, the case improves

This is especially relevant because decipherments often begin from proper names, as Mosenkis emphasizes.

### G3. Place names are a legitimate foothold
Mosenkis leans heavily on toponymic identification, and that is not inherently ridiculous.

Examples already accepted or near-accepted in the project:
- `pa-i-to` ↔ Phaistos
- `ku-do-ni` / `ku-do-ni-ja` ↔ Kydonia / Khania
- `su-ki-ri-ta` ↔ Sybrita
- `tu-ru-sa` ↔ Tylissos
- `se-to-i-ja` ↔ Siteia?

A Greek advocate can say:
> if the corpus preserves a large layer of place names that later survive in Greek, this may indicate continuity between the language of the tablets and later Greek place-name traditions, rather than total language replacement.

That argument is not decisive, but it is stronger than pure word-cherry-picking.

### G4. Orthographic mismatch is not by itself fatal
This is Mosenkis's best methodological argument.

He argues that:
- Linear A orthography need not match Linear B orthography exactly
- differences in vowel marking, final vowels, and sign usage could reflect orthographic conventions rather than different languages
- Linear B itself differs from Cypriot Syllabic despite both writing Greek

This point matters. The anti-Greek case cannot simply say:
> "Linear A doesn't look exactly like Linear B, therefore not Greek."

That is too easy and not rigorous enough.

### G5. Some commodity / object-based quasi-bilingual proposals are at least testable
From `Searching_bilinguals.docx`, Mosenkis proposes cases like:
- `a-ka-ru OLE` ↔ Greek `agrios` / wild olive context
- `ku-pa3- ... OLE` ↔ Greek `kyparos` / plant term
- object-word associations like words on axes, lamps, jars

Most of these are weak individually. But steelmanned, the argument is:
> if Linear A labels occur in recurrent object contexts that match plausible Greek lexemes, then at least some semantic identifications might point toward Greek vocabulary.

Again: not proof, but a testable route.

### G6. Greek may preserve substrate or dialectal irregularity
The strongest Greek case is probably **not** "Linear A is ordinary Mycenaean Greek."  
It is something more like:

> Linear A could encode an early, dialectally distinct, heavily substrate-influenced, orthographically unconventional form of Greek, or a Greek-contact koine in which many later Mycenaean conventions were not yet stabilized.

That is a much stronger version than the naive Greek hypothesis.

## 2. Best counter-arguments to the project's anti-Greek claims

### Against: "The morphology is wrong"
Steelman response:
- We may be over-reading agglutination from a script with weak vowel resolution and formula-heavy material
- some apparent suffix stacks may involve compounding, enclitics, frozen titles, or orthographic segmentation problems
- Greek dialects and pre-standard orthographies can look less transparently fusional in syllabic writing than later alphabetic Greek

This is the best possible response, though it is still under pressure from the actual suffix-chaining evidence.

### Against: "The vocabulary has no Greek etymologies"
Steelman response:
- the corpus is short, damaged, and mostly administrative
- administrative shorthand is a terrible domain for easy etymologizing
- proper names and toponyms often provide the first real foothold
- some project judgments may be too strict in excluding phonologically distorted Greek possibilities

This is fair as a caution, especially against overconfidence.

### Against: "ja-sa-sa-ra-me is Luwian, not Greek"
Steelman response:
- one Luwian or Anatolian borrowing does not make the language non-Greek
- religious vocabulary is especially borrowable
- a Greek-speaking or Greek-adjacent ritual elite could still use Anatolian cult vocabulary

This is actually a strong counterpoint. A Luwian ritual borrowing is perfectly compatible with Greek contact.

### Against: "extra signs imply non-Greek phonemes"
Steelman response:
- scripts often preserve signs for values that are no longer phonemic or are used logographically/specialized
- Linear A may reflect orthographic distinctions that do not correspond neatly to language-family boundaries
- unusual signs do not automatically disprove Greek without a firmer phonological reconstruction

Again: not a win for Greek, but a fair caution.

## 3. What Mosenkis contributes that should not be dismissed too quickly

The Mosenkis materials are often overreaching, but three things are methodologically useful:

### M1. Proper names and toponyms really do matter
This is historically true in decipherment work. The instinct is legitimate.

### M2. Orthographic conservatism can mislead
He is right that writing-system mismatch does not by itself settle language identity.

### M3. Greek hypotheses should be tested adversarially, not mocked away
A serious verifier should try to rescue the Greek case before rejecting it. That part of the assignment is worthwhile.

## 4. Why the Greek steelman still falls short

After granting the strongest possible case, the Greek hypothesis still has major problems.

### F1. The morphology problem is still the biggest obstacle
The project's current evidence for:
- multiple productive suffixes
- chained suffixes
- root stability under suffix addition
- lack of visible Greek-style fusional paradigms

remains hard to reconcile with Greek as the primary language of the corpus.

A steelman can weaken certainty, but it does not remove the problem.

### F2. Most Mosenkis proposals are too permissive
The toponymic matches often rely on:
- broad phonetic flexibility
- multiple unproven orthographic substitutions
- selective use of possible Greek parallels

That makes them useful as prompts, but weak as proof.

### F3. Shared place names do not prove shared language identity
Toponyms are the easiest things to survive across language shifts.

So even if `pa-i-to`, `ku-do-ni`, and `su-ki-ri-ta` are secure, that does **not** prove the underlying language of Linear A administration was Greek.

### F4. The strongest non-toponymic anchors still resist Greek
The best-understood project anchors remain things like:
- `ku-ro`
- `ki-ro`
- `sa-ra`
- the productive suffix system
- `ja-sa-sa-ra-me`

These do not currently form a convincing Greek core.

### F5. The best Greek case is really a weaker claim than "Linear A is Greek"
The strongest survivable version is something like:

> Linear A may preserve some early Greek-adjacent, Greek-contact, or Greek-transferable vocabulary, especially in toponyms and possibly some object-terms, while the overall language still appears non-Greek.

That is far weaker than a true Greek decipherment claim.

---

## 5. Best possible Greek case, stated cleanly

If I had to state the strongest version of the Greek hypothesis after this steelman, it would be:

> Because Linear A and Linear B are related scripts, because some place names and perhaps a few lexical items appear continuous into later Greek, and because orthographic differences do not automatically imply a different language, it remains possible that Linear A preserves a stronger Greek-adjacent layer than the current project allows. Some words now treated as opaque may yet prove compatible with early Greek or with Greek shaped by strong substrate and Anatolian contact.

That is the strongest fair version.

---

## 6. Final assessment

### How strong is the Greek steelman actually?
**Moderate as a challenge to overconfidence. Weak as a full alternative explanation.**

In other words:
- the steelman succeeds in showing that the anti-Greek case should not be phrased lazily
- it does **not** succeed in making Greek the best explanation of the corpus

### Best verifier judgment
The Greek hypothesis is worth keeping as an adversarial test, especially via:
- toponym continuity
- LA-LB overlap review
- orthographic caution

But after steelmanning it, the current evidence still does **not** make Greek the most plausible overall explanation for the Linear A corpus.

## 7. Most useful next tests
If the project wants to keep Greek under fair pressure, the best next checks would be:
1. classify all 21 LA-LB parallels into:
   - place names
   - personal names
   - possible common vocabulary
2. directly test whether any non-name administrative terms have a better Greek explanation than the current non-Greek reading
3. isolate the strongest 5 Mosenkis proposals and test them against corpus distribution, not just phonetic resemblance

## Bottom line
The strongest case for Greek is not that Linear A straightforwardly records Greek. It is that the script, some toponyms, and some lexical continuities may preserve a **Greek-adjacent layer** worth testing. That is a respectable adversarial position. But on current evidence, it still falls short of displacing the project's present conclusion that the core language behind Linear A is unlikely to be Greek.
