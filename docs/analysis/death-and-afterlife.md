# The Death and Afterlife of Linear A

## Three Acts

### Act 1: The Death of the Script (~1450 BCE)

Around 1450 BCE, a wave of destructions swept across Crete. Every major palatial center burned: Haghia Triada, Phaistos, Zakros, Khania, Mallia, Gournia, Palaikastro. Only **Knossos survived** — and there, Linear A was replaced by **Linear B**, the same signs repurposed to write Greek.

The administrative death was **instantaneous**. When the palaces burned, the bureaucracy that needed the script burned with it. The archives at every major site were sealed by destruction at the end of Late Minoan IB.

**Cause:** Most likely a Mycenaean Greek military takeover. The appearance of Linear B (writing Greek) at Knossos immediately after the destructions, combined with changes in burial practices and material culture, points to conquest. The Theran eruption (Santorini) occurred earlier (~1628 BCE) and was not the direct cause.

**The key datum:** After the destructions, Knossos wrote in Linear B. Every other palace that used Linear A was abandoned or reduced. One dominant center, one new script, one new language. This is conquest.

### Act 2: The Slow Death of the Language (~1450–~300 BCE)

The Minoan language did not die with its script. It **retreated to eastern Crete**, away from Mycenaean power centers.

**Evidence of survival:**

**~1350 BCE** — The Poros Herakleiou figurine bears a painted variant of the libation formula containing **sa-sa-ra** (the core of ja-sa-sa-ra-me, "My Lady"). Someone was still writing Linear A for ritual purposes **100 years** after the administrative script died.

**~1350 BCE** — Two Khania inscriptions (KH 102, a roundel) are dated LM IIIA, suggesting some Linear A administrative use persisted alongside Linear B at this western Cretan site.

**7th–3rd century BCE** — At **Dreros** and **Praisos** in eastern Crete, inscriptions written in the **Greek alphabet** but in a **non-Greek language** appear. These are the **Eteocretan** ("true Cretan") inscriptions. The language:
- Is not Indo-European
- Is not Semitic
- Has no obvious relation to any known language
- May be a direct descendant of the Minoan language

Herodotus (5th century BCE) knew of the "Eteocretans" as one of the indigenous peoples of Crete, placing them in exactly the eastern region where these inscriptions were found.

**The Eteocretan question is the single most important unsolved connection in this project.** If Eteocretan IS the descendant of Minoan, then we have the same language written in two different scripts 1,000 years apart — and the Greek alphabet version might be readable.

### Act 3: The Afterlife (~300 BCE–present)

The language died, but its ghosts remain.

**Substrate words in Greek:**
- Place names ending in **-ssos** (Knossos, Halicarnassus), **-nthos** (Corinth, labyrinth, Zakynthos), **-ttos** (Lyktos) — characteristically pre-Greek, not Indo-European
- Plant/animal names: possibly **kyparissos** (cypress), **narkissos** (narcissus)
- Cultural terms: **labyrinthos** (labyrinth) — the most famous Minoan word surviving in modern languages worldwide

**Religious continuity:**
- i-da-ma-te ("Mother Ida") → Greek **Rhea/Cybele** cult centered on Mount Ida
- The Minoan mother goddess tradition continued into Phrygian Matar worship and Roman Mater Idaea
- Every time someone says "labyrinth," they are speaking Minoan

**The parallel:** Schoep showed that Cretan Hieroglyphic and Linear A coexisted for 200 years representing different political centers. The LA→LB transition followed the same pattern but more violently — a political replacement, not a gradual evolution. The script changed because the rulers changed. The language took centuries longer to die.
