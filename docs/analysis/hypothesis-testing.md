# Linear A Language Hypotheses: Corpus-Based Analysis

**Date:** April 2026
**Method:** Systematic testing of decipherment hypotheses against the unified corpus (1,880 documents), Younger's Lexicon (1,463 word forms), and the project glossary (52 entries with confidence scores).

---

## Executive Summary

Four major hypotheses have been proposed for the language behind Linear A: **Hurrian**, **Greek**, **Semitic**, and **language isolate**. We tested each against the morphological structure of the corpus, the vocabulary of the best-understood administrative terms, the sign inventory, and the libation formula.

**Results:**
- **Semitic** can be substantially weakened — the morphology is incompatible
- **Greek** can be weakened — the morphology is wrong, the core vocabulary has no Greek etymologies, and the best-analyzed ritual phrase points to Luwian
- **Hurrian** has the best morphological fit but fails on vocabulary — the most common LA words have no Hurrian cognates
- **An unknown agglutinative language** (possibly with Anatolian/Luwian connections in its religious register) remains the most parsimonious explanation

---

## 1. Morphological Structure

### 1.1 Word Length Distribution

From 1,463 tokens in Younger's Lexicon:

| Syllables | Count | % |
|-----------|-------|------|
| 2 | 616 | 42.1% |
| 3 | 496 | 33.9% |
| 4 | 233 | 15.9% |
| 5+ | 118 | 8.1% |

Average: **2.95 syllables/word**. The 8% tail of 5+ syllable words (including 8-syllable forms like JA-DI-KI-TE-TE-DU-PU₂-RE) is consistent with occasional agglutination.

### 1.2 Suffix System

The corpus shows **6-8 productive suffixes** that attach to noun stems:

| Suffix | Freq | Proposed function |
|--------|------|-------------------|
| -ja | 85 (5.8%) | Adjectival/ethnic |
| -re | 70 (4.8%) | Agent/category |
| -ti | 70 (4.8%) | Case marker? |
| -na | 68 (4.6%) | Genitive/agentive |
| -te | 63 (4.3%) | Dative/locative |
| -ra | 59 (4.0%) | Agent/category |
| -si | 49 (3.3%) | Unknown |
| -ne | 42 (2.9%) | Dative plural? |
| -se | 42 (2.9%) | Instrumental/ablative? |

**14 roots** show 3+ different final syllables attached to the same stem (e.g., A-TA- takes -DE, -I, -NA, -NATE, -RE). This looks like a **case paradigm**.

### 1.3 Suffix Chaining (Critical Finding)

166 cases found where shorter attested words appear as the beginning of longer words:

- TA-NA → TA-NA-TE → TA-NA-SU-TE
- DA-KU → DA-KU-SE-NE → DA-KU-SE-NE-TI
- U-NA → U-NA-KA → U-NA-KA-NA → U-NA-KA-NA-SI
- KI-TA → KI-TA-NA-SI-JA-SE (root + 4 suffixes)

This is **textbook agglutination** — suffixes stack progressively without modifying the stem. This is the single most diagnostic morphological feature in the corpus.

### 1.4 Prefix Patterns

Almost **no evidence of productive prefixation**. Only 4 word-bodies had 3+ different initial syllables, and these are unconvincing (likely different words). The morphology is **exclusively suffixal**.

### 1.5 Reduplication

47 reduplicated forms found:
- Full: TA-TA, JA-JA, DI-DI
- Partial: SA-RA-RA, QA-QA-RU, KI-KI-RA-JA
- In formulas: JA-SA-SA-RA-ME

Reduplication is nearly universal across language families and is not diagnostic. It is consistent with Hurrian (asti-asti "women"), Greek (le-luka), and Semitic (qatqata).

## 2. Testing Each Hypothesis

### 2.1 Hurrian Hypothesis (van Soesbergen)

| Test | Prediction | Observed | Result |
|------|-----------|----------|--------|
| Voicing neutralization | No b/p, g/k, d/t distinction | b- and g- absent, but **d/t both exist** | ⚠️ Partial |
| Agglutinative morphology | Suffix chaining, no prefixes | **Confirmed** | ✅ |
| 6-8 case suffixes | Matches Hurrian 8-9 | **Confirmed** | ✅ |
| Ergative case marker | -se = ergative | Plausible (-se at 2.9%) | ⚠️ Possible |
| Hurrian vocabulary | Core admin terms match Hurrian | **ku-ro, ki-ro, sa-ra have NO Hurrian cognates** | ❌ |
| Libation formula | Hurrian prayer | Depends on undeciphered *301; Luwian reading is simpler | ❌ |
| Personal names | Hurrian sentence names | Too short to diagnose | ⚠️ Ambiguous |
| Place names | Hurrian toponyms | Substrate names, prove nothing | ⚠️ Neutral |

**Assessment:** Morphologically the best fit, but the vocabulary evidence is devastating. The most common, best-understood words in the entire corpus have no Hurrian etymologies. A language that IS Hurrian should have Hurrian words for "total," "deficit," and "assessment." It doesn't.

### 2.2 Greek Hypothesis (Mosenkis)

| Test | Prediction | Observed | Result |
|------|-----------|----------|--------|
| Fusional case endings | -os, -a, -e pattern | **Not found** — suffixes chain 4-5 deep | ❌ |
| Nominative -o | Productive bare -o ending | **Not found** — -ro exists but is a morpheme, not a case marker | ❌ |
| Gender alternation | -o/-a masculine/feminine | No evidence of systematic gender marking | ❌ |
| 5 cases | Greek has nom/gen/dat/acc/voc | **6-8 suffixes found** — too many for Greek | ❌ |
| Articles | Greek uses articles | **None found** | ❌ |
| Admin vocabulary | Greek etymologies for core terms | **0-2 of ~10 terms have even speculative etymologies** | ❌ |
| Libation formula | Greek religious vocab | **ja-sa-sa-ra-me = Luwian ishassara, NOT Greek** | ❌ |
| Extra signs | Same phoneme set as Greek | **LA has signs with no LB equivalent** (*301, *188) | ❌ |
| a-ka-ru = agrion | Wild olive oil | Distribution says name/heading, not commodity | ❌ |

**Assessment: Actively contradicted by corpus data.** The morphology is wrong (fusional vs. agglutinative), the vocabulary is wrong (no Greek etymologies), the best-analyzed phrase is Luwian, and the sign inventory implies non-Greek phonemes. The Greek hypothesis relies on cherry-picking individual words while ignoring systematic evidence.

### 2.3 Semitic Hypothesis (Gordon and others)

| Test | Prediction | Observed | Result |
|------|-----------|----------|--------|
| Root-and-pattern | Trilateral roots with vowel alternation | **Not found** | ❌ |
| Productive prefixes | Verb prefixes (ya-, ta-, a-) | **Not found** — morphology is exclusively suffixal | ❌ |
| Case vowels | -u, -a, -i on nouns | CV-syllable suffixes instead | ❌ |
| Suffix chaining | Not a Semitic feature | **Found** — incompatible | ❌ |

**Assessment: Substantially disproved.** The exclusively suffixal, agglutinative morphology is fundamentally incompatible with Semitic language structure. This is the one hypothesis that can be rejected with the most confidence.

### 2.4 Language Isolate / Unknown Agglutinative

| Test | Prediction | Observed | Result |
|------|-----------|----------|--------|
| Agglutinative morphology | Would fit various families | **Confirmed** | ✅ |
| Unique vocabulary | No matches to known languages | **Core terms have no etymologies** | ✅ |
| Luwian religious borrowings | Contact with Anatolian world | **ja-sa-sa-ra-me = Luwian** | ✅ |
| Substrate place names | Survived into Greek | **Confirmed** (pa-i-to, ku-do-ni-ja) | ✅ |

**Assessment: Most parsimonious.** An unknown agglutinative language — possibly related to Hurrian-Urartian, possibly an isolate — with Anatolian/Luwian loanwords in its religious register. This explains why the morphology looks Hurrian-like but the vocabulary doesn't match: the languages may be typologically similar without being genetically related.

## 3. The Sign Inventory Evidence

The Linear A sign grid lacks b- and g- series entirely:

| Series | Signs present | Signs absent |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| Labial | pa, pe, pi, po, pu | ba, be, bi, bo, bu |
| Velar | ka, ke, ki, ko, ku | ga, ge, gi, go, gu |
| Dental | **da, de, di, do, du AND ta, te, ti, to, tu** | — |

The selective neutralization (labials/velars merge, dentals don't) is important. If the script simply reflected a language without voicing distinctions, all three series should merge. The d/t split suggests the Minoan language DID distinguish d from t but NOT b from p or g from k — a phonological profile that doesn't match Hurrian (which neutralizes all three) or Greek (which distinguishes all three).

Linear A also has signs with no Linear B equivalent (*301, *188, *56, *325), implying phonemes that Greek doesn't have. This is strong evidence against the Greek hypothesis and suggests the Minoan language had a phonological system distinct from any proposed candidate.

## 4. The Libation Formula

The standard formula on stone offering vessels:

```
a-ta-i-*301-wa-ja | u-na-ka-na-si | i-pi-na-ma | si-ru-te |
ja-sa-sa-ra-me | u-na-ru-ka-na-ti | i-da-ma-te
```

**ja-sa-sa-ra-me** is the most analyzed term. The Luwian reading (ishassara-me = "my lady") is phonologically nearly exact and morphologically transparent (-me as possessive is independently attested in administrative contexts as da-me). The Hurrian reading (prayer to divine trinity) requires assigning divine names to sequences containing an undeciphered sign and is less parsimonious. The Greek reading has no plausible candidate.

**i-da-ma-te** ("Mother of Ida" or "Mother Ida") contains i-da (Mt. Ida, a pre-Greek toponym) and ma-te (cognate with PIE *méh₂tēr "mother"). The "mother" element has Indo-European cognates but is one of the most commonly shared words across language families. The compound structure is consistent with Anatolian naming patterns.

## 5. Conclusions

### What we can say with confidence:

1. **The Minoan language is agglutinative.** Suffix chaining 4-5 deep, no productive prefixes, 6-8 case suffixes. This is the firmest structural conclusion from the corpus.

2. **It is not Greek.** The morphological structure is wrong (agglutinative vs. fusional), the core vocabulary has no Greek etymologies, and the best-analyzed ritual phrase is Luwian, not Greek.

3. **It is not Semitic.** The absence of root-and-pattern morphology and productive prefixation rules out Semitic with high confidence.

4. **It may or may not be Hurrian.** The morphological fit is good but the vocabulary fit is poor. The most common words have no Hurrian cognates.

5. **It has Anatolian/Luwian connections.** The libation formula contains at least one Luwian word (ja-sa-sa-ra-me = ishassara + possessive). This suggests cultural/religious contact with the Anatolian world, consistent with Crete's geographic position.

6. **The language family remains unknown.** An agglutinative language with Anatolian loanwords in its religious register — possibly related to Hurrian-Urartian, possibly an isolate — is the most parsimonious explanation that fits all the evidence.

### What would change this:

- A bilingual inscription (Linear A + any known language) would be decisive
- The RILA Supplement's 107 new documents might contain longer texts enabling deeper analysis
- AI-powered cross-script sign comparison could identify shared sign values beyond the Linear B parallels
- New archaeological discoveries — particularly at ritual sites — could provide contextual clues
