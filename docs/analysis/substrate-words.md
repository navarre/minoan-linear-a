# Minoan Words Hiding in Greek

## The Fossils

When a language dies, some of its words survive in the language that replaced it — like Native American place names in English (Massachusetts, Connecticut, Mississippi). The Minoan language left hundreds of such fossils in Greek.

### Confirmed: DU-PU₂-RE → Labyrinthos

The single best-attested Minoan word surviving in a modern language.

| Stage | Form | Meaning | Evidence |
|-------|------|---------|----------|
| **Linear A** | DU-PU₂-RE | "lord, master" | PK Za 8, 11, 15; AP Za 3; KO Za 1b |
| **Linear B** | da-pu₂-ri-to | (Knossos) | With Greek -to suffix added |
| **Greek** | labyrinthos | "labyrinth" | d→l shift (Beekes' pre-Greek rule) |

The word originally meant **"house of the lord"** — the palace at Knossos. "Labyrinth" as "maze" is a later Greek reinterpretation. The d→l shift between Minoan and Greek is a known feature of pre-Greek substrate words.

Every time anyone in the world says "labyrinth," they are speaking Minoan.

### Strong: KU-PA-RI → Kyparissos (cypress)

| Stage | Form | Evidence |
|-------|------|----------|
| **Linear A** | ku-pa-ri | Place name at HT 1, 49, 88, 117, 122; PH |
| **Linear A** | ku-pa₃-ri-ja | Ethnic/adjectival form (with -ja suffix) |
| **Greek** | kyparissos | Cypress tree. No IE etymology. |

The Greek -issos suffix is absent in the LA form — it was added during Hellenization. The tree may have taken its name from a place, or vice versa.

### The Suffix Key

Pre-Greek suffixes in Greek words map to Linear A morphology:

| Greek suffix | Examples | LA equivalent | Explanation |
|-------------|----------|---------------|-------------|
| **-nthos** | labyrinthos, Korinthos, hyakinthos | Hidden — LA syllabary can't write -nth- clusters | Prenasalized stops appear as simple stops in LA writing |
| **-ssos/-ssa** | Knossos, kyparissos, narkissos | **-sa** | Greek geminate -ss- may represent Minoan -s- |
| **-na/-ne** | many | **-na/-ne** | Direct match. Highly productive in LA (59 occurrences). Same function. |
| **-ja** | ethnic forms | **-ja** | Identical function — marks ethnic/adjectival derivation in both LA and Greek pre-Greek words |

### Beekes' Pre-Greek Features vs. Linear A

Robert Beekes identified phonological rules for pre-Greek substrate words. They match what we know about Minoan:

| Beekes' rule | What it predicts | What LA shows | Match? |
|-------------|-----------------|---------------|--------|
| Prenasalized stops (mb, nd, ng) | Hidden in CV syllabary | DU-PU₂-RE → labyr-**inth**-os shows the nasal | Yes |
| d/l alternation | d- in substrate → l- in Greek | DU-PU₂-RE → **l**abyrinthos | Yes |
| s-mobile | Initial s- appears/disappears | su-ki-ri-ta / ki-re-ta- alternation | Possible |
| a/o alternation | Vowels unstable across dialects | Some LA words show vowel variation | Possible |

## What This Means

The pre-Greek substrate in Greek is not random borrowing — it follows systematic phonological rules that map directly to Linear A orthographic conventions. This is strong circumstantial evidence that:

1. The language behind Linear A IS the source of the pre-Greek substrate
2. The Minoan language was spoken widely enough to leave hundreds of words in Greek
3. The phonological "rules" Beekes identified for pre-Greek words are actually rules of the Minoan sound system as filtered through Greek ears
4. The LA syllabary's limitations (can't write clusters, prenasalized stops, certain consonants) explain why some Minoan sounds appear "garbled" in Greek borrowings
